Before you begin...

Here are some of the lessons we learned (the hard way) in building our sites. We suggest you use this advice. We're not gurus, but we did learn the trial by fire way and are now branded by our mistakes. We'll spare you the pain.

1. Don't even think that you can save this project for the week before it is due. You really have to be willing to work-- not only on content but on web-literacy as well. If you commit yourself to it, this site will be a great learning opportunity and you'll use it in the future. If not, it will become one big, frustrating,"this assignment is so dumb" pain in your neck. (And you 'll never use it again...what a shame because it could land you quite the nice job.)

2. You're not going to have an epiphany right when you sit down at the computer. Before you even touch a keyboard, review websites that you like (especially design wise). Think extensively about the "look" of your site, and draw it on paper. Make a list of what will go on each page (check the requirements page to see if you've got it all) and decide how you will navigate through your site. Haven't you ever been to a site that you know contains something but you don't know where to go to get it? Don't do that to your visitors!

3. Spend some time surfing for backgrounds and images relating to your theme. That way you know that what you want is out there. You could spend YEARS searching for bigger and better images, so just resign yourself to the fact that you will never be "done." Your website is a work in progress.

4. Take your drafts to the IT lab in Sangren Hall and they help you master Dreamweaver. This is the ONLY program that the IT lab endorses, so we highly encourage it. You can download Dreamweaver, but only for 30 days. So use your time wisely.

5. Have access to a zip disk to make sure all of your info is in one place. If you keep changing your source you'll have to re-link all of the pictures, backgrounds, images, etc. on your site. KEEP INFORMATION ON MORE THAN ONE DISK. Things happen.

6. Get the basics done first. If you spend a ton of time loading pictures onto your personal page, changes are you are neglecting one of the essentials, like your educational philosophy or your parent links.

7. Know what facilities each lab has and when it's open. If you have a simple task to take care of, go to Brown, Khorman, or Haworth. If you need to scan or use special programs, allot yourself specific times in your schedule to go. Oh, and if you are a MAC user you might have to dig a little. Erin found that the Gilmore Theatre Complex is complete with scanners and design equipment, and helpful design majors. You won't just "happen" to have web building time in your schedule. Trust us.

8. Give yourself deadlines. Work on one page at a time, and you'll feel like you are making progress.

9. We recommend using tables--not layers. Our experience is that layers will move in different browsers, ruining the look of your site. You can "convert layers to tables" as a function in Dreamweaver, but it doesn't always work. Layers do have the advantage of allowing you to locate images and text on the page exactly where you want it.

10. When scanning pictures, always make sure the resolution is 72. Also, enter in your destination size before you scan so that all of your pictures will be uniform, and you won't have to resize and distort them in your design program. This gives pictures that pixelized look--boo. Photoshop has a nice "save for the web" featuer that allows you to preview how your images will look before you save them and also set them at a size that will download quickly.

11. If you create images in Adobe Illustrator, make sure you are using "web-colors." This limits your options significantly, and you don't want to get too attached to an image and find out it won't load.

12. Be as prepared as you can when coming to campus to work. Have everything drawn, written, and saved to disk so all you have to do is enter it into Dreamweaver. Don't waste your time in the IT lab searching for backgrounds. This will really piss off the people waiting for real help.

13. Even if you come prepared, you are going to be amazed at the amount of time it takes to put this all together. Plan on AT LEAST 4 hours per page.

14. BEG, BORROW, and STEAL. Use Allen's site, other teacher/professor's sites, and other students' sites to get great links and ideas. BUT be sure to give credit where credit is due. Stealing designs, backgrounds, images is illegal and rude. We don't condone meanies.

15. Do as much as you can early in the semester. The IT lab gets REALLY busy toward the end of the semester due to the abundance of procrastination at this university. Don't be a slacker.
