Spring 2005/ English 597: Special Topics
Literature on the Web

Rough Draft Syllabus!


This experimental course will consider the impact of cyberculture on reading, writing, and learning. We will critically examine hypertext writing, internet publishing, electronic text archives, and on-line literature resources. We will consider cyber-science fiction and film, collaborative on-line environments, and computer games as narratives. The final project will be an open-ended opportunity for students to develop websites to experiment with writing hyperfiction, explore specific literary works or authors, and/or create literature teaching resources.

The class will be taught in the English Department's new wireless, laptop classroom. Literature on the Web should appeal both to students who are already interested in technology as well as to students who have little or no experience in this area but who are intrigued by literature and new approaches to studying and teaching it. Attendance and class participation are essential, missing more than four classes may lead to failing the class. Students can use the WMU Homepages server to create their sites.

Provisional Syllabus
 

Introduction to the Class
Order a work of hypertext fiction from Eastgate Systems
  Read: The Library of Babel by Borges, Essays: The Dream of Cyberspace, The Library of Babel and the Internet
Examine: Design Images, Atlas of Cyberspace

 

History of the Present
Read: "Origin of World Literature," Karl Marx
from Imagined Communities, Benedict Anderson
"Cyberspace" from CyberReader by Benjamin Wooley
"Bitmapping" from Interface Culture by Steven Johnson
  Imagining the Future
Read: Neuromancer, by William Gibson
Neuromancer Study Guide

 

 Hypertext Fiction
Hypertext Reading Assignment.
Browse hypertext fiction from Michael Schumante's Hypertext Fiction Index, look at the notes on Figurski at Findhorn on Acid.

Future of Narrative?
Read: The Garden of Forking Paths by Borges and Hamlet on the Holodeck, chapters 1-6
Examine Hamlet on the Holodeck Resource Page
Talk to Eliza!
Read: Finish Hamlet on the Holodeck

Literature on the Web: Websearch and Presentations
Browse Sites Such As: On-line Library Ezine Index,
Babel , Janet Murray's On-line Fiction Course
 

Virtual Communities: MOOS & MUDS
Study MOOs & MUDs, take on a character in the Brave New World MOO, Check out MOOS on line.
  Computer Games as Narrative
Examine such games as Myst, Sim City, Empires, Myth, Doom, Quake, etc.
 

Literature Archives
Read from Radiant Textuality, Introduction, Chap. 2 "The Rationale of Hypertext," Chapter 5 "Rethinking Textuality," Chapter 6 "Visible and Invisible Books in N-Dimensional Space," and Conclusion "The Ivanhoe Game" Look at on-line information on The Ivanhoe Game and at records from previous games played.
 

Scholarly Resources: Websearch & Presentations
Examine Archives such as: Rossetti Archive, Dickinson Electronic Archives, MIT Internet Classics, Rutgers Literary Resources on the Internet, Blake Archives, Bartleby, University of Virginia Electronic Text Center, Project Gutenberg, Early English Books On-line, Humanities Text Initiative, other on-line text resources.
Examine Resources such as: Voice of the Shuttle, Internet Resources in Literature, E-Server.org, Victorian Web, Postcolonial Literature in English, Postcolonial Dialogues, About.Com, BeyondBooks, Virtual English


Work on Final Projects
 

Course Reflection
Read:
from Growing Up Digital, Donald Tapscott
from Let Them Eat Data, Chet Bowers
from Tools for Conviviality, Ivan Illich

 

Final Exam
Describe the future with attention to literature on the web.
