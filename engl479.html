Autoethnography and the Teaching of Writing

Teaching writing can empower students to trust and value their own words and voice, to inquire more deeply into knowledge and ideas, to be creative, to better understand themselves and the world around them, and to speak out clearly and cogently to others on topics that matter.

Facilitating the power of writing will be the focus of this section of English 4790 Teaching Writing in the Secondary Schools. Students will write about their own experiences learning to write, learn about leading writing workshops, effective ways to improve student writing, and make it meaningful. We'll focus too on writing in the digital age and using new tools for revision, collaboration, and publication, including blogs, wikis, and nings.

Class will be held in a new, wireless, laptop classroom in Brown Hall specifically designed to prepare future teachers for the "classroom of the future." You'll enjoy this class and learn a great deal about possibilities for teaching writing.

More culturally self-conscious than "autobiography," the concept of "autoethnography" emphasizes understanding individual identity within cultural, social, and historical communities and frameworks. According to Mary Louise Pratt, autoethnographies offer an especially important way for marginal or subordinate groups to represent themselves and write back to established knowlwedge. This section of English 479 will draw on autoethnographies, both of teachers and students, as a way into thinking about how best to teach writing in the context of student's lives, to simultaneously develop student voice and socio-cultural sophistication.

The class will begin by exploring the concept of "autoethnography" and then turn to four professional teaching autoethnographies written by middle school, high school, and college writing teachers. We will focus on the diverse contexts in which they teach, the variety of approaches they use, and their own growth as teachers and community leaders. During this section of the course students will write their own autoethnography, focusing on their experiences as students of writing instruction. Next, working in groups, students will read a variety of autobiographical / autoethnographic works, in order, by the end of the semester, to develop a final autoethnographic curriculum project for the level at which they intend to teach. During this section of the course students will also make presentations to the class about particular problems in the teaching of writing. The last class meetings will provide an opportunity for critical reflection on what and how we have learned and the implications of this knowledge for intern teaching and beyond.

Throughout 479 there will be wide latitude for discussion and the expression of diverse opinions and perspectives. Integral to this process is a computer conference (ENGLISH-TEACHER) providing a complementary forum for examination of course goals, content, activity, and methodology. The conference can be accessed through many locations on campus or via modem; English 479 students have admission to the computer lab on the first floor of Brown Hall.

Part II Autoethnography and Teaching

Literature/Teaching Circles

In groups of at least four, students are expected to read in common one autobiography, autoethnography, autobiographical novel/poetry collection per week. These should be new works that students have not read previously. For every class meeting students are expected to prepare (and share with their group) one page worth of writing prompts, journal assignments, study questions, creative writing activities, research projects, think sheets, grading or edition rubric, discussion questions, tests, essay questions, lesson plan/materials, background/cultural information, letter to parents, etc. that they could be used in teaching the text under consideration in a secondary English classroom. After group critique, it is expected that these materials will be improved and modified before inclusion in the final teaching unit. Groups membership can vary from week to week.

Presentations on Teaching of Writing

During each class period students in groups of two will make 45 minute presentations/lessons on topics such as teaching writing to special education students, academic writing, teaching journalism, writing across the curriculum, writing in response to literature, 5-paragraph papers, talented and gifted writing instruction, composition theory, assigning homework, classroom management, paper load, job search, etc. depending on student interest.
