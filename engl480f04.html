English 480, Fall 2004

Teaching Literature in the Secondary Schools

Reader Response, Cultural Studies, & the Internet

This section of English 480 will ground students in traditional approaches to literature pedagogy while simultaneously focusing on recent waves of reform, reader response, cultural studies, and the impact of the internet. For over a generation the reader response movement has helped teachers better attend to the learning process of secondary students, yet, in confronting a wide range of students, content questions, and social and cultural issues, reader response leaves questions unanswered. Potential answers and new directions are emerging under the umbrella of "cultural studies." Most recently, dramatic changes in information technology extend and enrich the teaching of literature. For instance, the cultural archive now on-line far exceeds what is available in traditional textbooks. In this section of English 480, we will examine the legacy of reader response, the potential of cultural studies, and possibility of internet resources to exploring fundamental questions of why and how to teach literature.

Class will be held in a wireless, laptop classroom in Brown Hall specifically designed for English education courses. The English Education Lab will allow us to integrate technology into literature teaching in a "classroom of the future." Our course follows this on-line syllabus that also serves as an electronic, hyperlinked text book. All students will develop and publish their own teaching website, both an electronic portfolio of work and a real-world working site for future teaching. This project can become the basis of the portfolio required during intern teaching. Students will also develop state-of-the-art webquests for their future students. Obtain help for your technology projects at the IT Lab on the Tower Bridge of the second floor of the Computer Center.

Course discussions will be significantly extended in our own electronic conference, "English-Teacher." By the end of the semester you should input 36,000 characters for an "A" -- that will likely mean at least thirty minutes per week.

A significant portion of the class will be student-led, as we explore the development of response-based, cultural studies literature teaching within the context of NCTE and State of Michigan content standards. Student groups will select topics addressing current and controversial themes such as the mass media, literature and the environment, literature and the Third World, literature and religion, literature and sexual orientation, etc.

We will have an electronic computer conference that will provide a forum for examination of course goals, content, activity, and methodology as well as serving as a link to web resources. A substantial website focused on Teaching English Through Technology has been developed to support this class and will provide access to streaming videos, on-line resources, and our own server space. Dr. Webb's web site (www.allenwebb.net) is an additional resources for aspiring English teachers. You may want to examine the technology outcomes expected of WMU education students. Always bring floppy disks to class so you can retain copies of your work. Before the end of the course, I urge you to burn a copy of your individual and content websites and webquest on to a CD ROM.

As the capstone experience for English Education majors, this course entails an exciting variety of professional activities and responsibilities. Students are expected to join the professional organizations, NCTE and MCTE, and read regularly the English Journal or Voices from the Middle. You are also expected to attend at least one professional teacher conference during the semester. The MCTE Fall Conference in Lansing will also be held Friday, October 8(register on-line in advance). It is exciting that the NCTE National Conference will be held in Indianapolis November 18-21 -- attending would be a wonderful introduction to the profession! Information about the Michigan Teacher Certification test is available on the MTTC website.

This semester our class and our department will host the visit of Dr. Richard Beach from the University of Minnesota, one of the leading researchers in English education. He will visit our class on October 20 and make a special evening presentation at 7:30 that students from 480 are expected to attend.

The final course grade will be an average of grades for the major assignments, listed and weighted below. At the hour scheduled for the final exam students will attend an intern teaching panel. Class participation is vital in 480, missing more than 3 classes may lower the grade and missing more than 5 classes may lead to failing. This course will follow WMU policies regarding academic honesty. This course also supports the Safe on Campus environment (387-2123); The Last Closet is recommended reading for gay and straight future teachers.

Dr. Webb's office is 723 Sprau Tower, 387-2605, and his office hours are Monday and Wednesday after 3:15 and by email and confer. If at any point in the semester if you feel stress, English 480 offers free on-line therapy from Eliza! (One of the early products of artificial intelligence research.)
