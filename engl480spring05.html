English 480, Spring 2005

Teaching Literature in the Secondary Schools

Social Justice & Teaching Literature
in the Secondary Schools

Social justice issues have long been a concern of English language arts teachers and of the literature they teach. We will draw on this rich tradition as we bring to bear reader response, critical pedagogy, and cultural studies theories and approaches. Working in collaboration with sections of English 480 taught by Dan Baker and Shannon Mortimore, we will explore social justice as a primary theme for teaching literature. The experimental collaboration between these three classes -- and perhaps other methods courses across the country -- will provide synergy and enhance the learning community.

Class will be held in a wireless, laptop classroom in Brown Hall specifically designed for English education courses. The English Education Lab will allow us to integrate technology into literature teaching in a "classroom of the future." Our course follows this on-line syllabus that also serves as an electronic, hyperlinked text book. All students will develop and publish their own teaching website, both an electronic portfolio of work and a real-world working site for future teaching. This project can become the basis of the portfolio required during intern teaching. Obtain help for your technology projects at the IT Lab on the Tower Bridge of the second floor of the Computer Center.

A significant portion of the class will be student-led, as we explore the development of response-based, cultural studies literature teaching within the context of NCTE and State of Michigan content standards. Student groups will addressing generative themes relevant to English studies and social justice including gender and sexual orientation, religion and literature, race and white priviledge in America, Native American studies, the "Third World," service learning.

A substantial website focused on Teaching English Through Technology has been developed to support this class and will provide access to streaming videos, on-line resources, and our own server space. Dr. Webb's web site (www.allenwebb.net) is an additional resources for aspiring English teachers. You may want to examine the technology outcomes expected of WMU education students. Always bring floppy disks to class so you can retain copies of your work. Before the end of the course, I urge you to burn a copy of your individual and content websites and webquest on to a CD ROM.

As the capstone experience for English Education majors, this course entails an exciting variety of professional activities and responsibilities. Students are expected to join the professional organizations, NCTE and MCTE, and read regularly the English Journal or Voices from the Middle. There will be a variety of activities outside of regular class time. There will be a technology panel on Jan 12, a workshop on teaching about civil rights Jan 19 during MLK week, and a number of special speakers coming to the English Departmetn during the semester (attend at least three). You are also expected to attend the Bright Ideas Conference in Lansing on Saturday, April 16th (register on-line in advance). Presenting at a poster session at this conference (!) will take the place of the regular take-home exam.

Course discussions will be significantly extended in an electronic conference that we will share with other sections of English 480 to develop an inclusive and mutually supportive teaching community. By the end of the semester you should input 36,000 characters for an "A" -- that will likely mean at least thirty minutes per week. Participation in the electronic conference and in the professional activities listed above are all included as part of class participation.

The final course grade will be an average of grades for the major assignments, listed and weighted below. At the hour scheduled for the final exam students will attend an intern teaching panel. Class participation is vital in 480, missing more than 3 classes may lower the grade and missing more than 5 classes may lead to failing. This course will follow WMU policies regarding academic honesty. This course also supports the Safe on Campus environment (387-2123); The Last Closet is recommended reading for gay and straight future teachers. Information about the Michigan Teacher Certification test is available on the MTTC website.

Dr. Webb's office is 723 Sprau Tower, 387-2605, and his office hours are Wednesday from 12 to 2 and by email and confer. If at any point in the semester if you feel stress, English 480 offers free on-line therapy from Eliza! (One of the early products of artificial intelligence research.)

Planning the Course, Individual and Group Expectations, formation of groups

Jan. 10, Mon.: Workshop Classroom Website

1. Read carefully through the entire on-line syllabus, including all assignments. Bring any questions about these assignments to class. Be sure you know what each page of a classroom website should contain.

2. Read all of the "Contents" pages on the Growing up Digital.What is the "N-generation and what are its characteristics? How do they approach teaching and learning differently?

2. Think about the role of technology in the future of English teaching. View the streaming video and carefully study information on classroom websites on the "Teaching with Technology" page of the Teaching English through Technology website.

3. Join and respond to questions on the class computer conference.

4. Obtain unified computer account from the computer center and bring account name and password to class. Register for your own website at WMU homepages.

Jan. 12, Wed.: Lesson Planning Resources

1. Read three or more articles from back issues of the English Journal that interest you. You should subscribe to the English Journal and you can do so from its home website and view the current issue. NCTE members can read back issues of the English Journal at the NCTE website on-line using their membership number as a password. Back issues are also in the Sangren Library under the call number PE1 .E5. (Between Waldo (all issues before 1980) and Sangren (issues after 1980) we have back issues to 1912 when the English Journal began publication--fascinating reading in the history of secondary English teaching!) You can conduct an ERIC search for articles on specific topics, for example for the unit you will be leading, by setting "English Journal" as the "source."

2. Find three or more secondary English lesson plans available on the web that you consider to be thoughtful and well-crafted. There are many sources for Language Arts lesson plans on the web. Try Outta Ray's Head, the Collaborative Lesson Archive, NCTE's Notes Plus (subscribers only), New York Times Lesson Plan Archive, Lesson Planz.com, Cyberguides, Lesson Plans Page, ERIC, etc.

3. Drawing on at least three on-line lesson plans and three English Journal articles, develop an annotated list of "Teaching Ideas" with at least a paragraph of description and comments about how you might use or modify the idea in your own classroom. This will be one page for the teacher resources section of your website. Bring to class on a floppy disk.
