English 480, Summer 2005

Teaching Literature in the Secondary Schools

Teaching Literature for Social Justice

This section of English 480 will ground students in traditional approaches to literature pedagogy while simultaneously focusing on recent waves of reform, reader response, cultural studies, and the impact of the internet. We will use a thematic approach to integrate these approaches as we use the focus of "Teaching Literature for Social Justice" to explore a variety of social justice themes in a problem-posing, student-led format.

For over a generation the reader response movement has generated reform in secondary English teaching. Yet, in confronting a wide range of students, content questions, and social and cultural issues, reader response approaches fall short. Potential answers and new directions for English teaching are beginning to emerge under the umbrella of "cultural studies" and these approaches forground consideration of social justice questions. In addition, changes in information technology are offering to extend and reshape the teaching of literature. The inherited cultural archive is now available on-line in formats and with complementary resources that far exceed what has been available in traditional textbooks. We will examine the legacy of reader response and the potential of cultural studies and internet resources for exploring fundamental questions of why and how to teach literature. By focusing on difficult and potentially controversial issues, students will gain an understanding of the issues involved in teaching literature at the secondary level.

Class will be held in a wireless, laptop classroom in Brown Hall specifically designed for English education courses. The English Education Lab will allow us to integrate technology into literature teaching in a "classroom of the future." Our class will be organized by this on-line syllabus that will also serve as an electronic, hyperlinked, textbook. All students will develop and publish their own teaching website, both a portfolio of work and a real-world working site for future teaching. This website can serve as the basis of the portfolio required during intern teaching. Obtain help for your technology projects at the IT Lab on the Tower Bridge of the second floor of the Computer Center.

A significant portion of the class will be student-led, as we explore the development of response-based, cultural studies literature teaching within the context of NCTE and the State of Michigan content standards and benchmarks. Student groups will select topics addressing current and controversial themes such as the literature and the Third World, literature and the environment, literature and social class, literature and religion, literature and sexual orientation, literature and service learning, literature and the mass media, teaching Native American literature, literature and white priviledge, etc.

Course discussions will be significantly extended in the class on-line discussion forum on the "English-Teacher" computer conference. Participation in the electronic conference and in the professional activities are all included as part of class participation.

A substantial website focused on Teaching English Through Technology has been developed to support this class and will provide access to streaming videos, on-line resources, and our own server space. Dr. Webb's website (www.allenwebb.net) is an additional resources for aspiring English teachers. You may want to examine the technology outcomes expected of WMU education students. Bring floppy disks to class so you can retain copies of your work. Before the end of the course, I urge you to burn a copy of your individual and content websites and webquest to a CD ROM.

As the capstone experience for English Education majors, this course entails an exciting variety of professional activities and responsibilities. Students are expected to join the professional organizations, NCTE and MCTE, and read regularly the English Journal or Voices from the Middle. Information about the Michigan Teacher Certification test is available on the MTTC website.

It is essential that you plan significant time to do the homework for this class. There will be substantial reading, course work, and meeting with student groups outside of class time. This course will require two to three hours of work outside of class per hour spent in class. Given that it is a double speed, 4-credit course, and that we are meeting 7 hours per week, that is an additional 14-21 hours per week of homework time. It will simply not be possible to be successful in 480 during this highly condensed summer semester if you are taking other courses or working many hours.

Class participation is vital in 480 as one class meeting of a summer course equals a full week during the regular semester. Missing more than 2 classes will lower the grade and missing more than 3 classes may lead to failing.

The final course grade will be an average of grades for the major assignments, listed and weighted below. At the hour scheduled for the final exam students will attend an intern teaching panel. This course will follow WMU policies regarding academic honesty. This course also supports the Safe on Campus environment (387-2123); The Last Closet is recommended reading for gay and straight future teachers.

Dr. Webb's office is 723 Sprau Tower, 387-2605, and he will typically be available for office hours by request after class. If at any point in the semester if you feel stress, English 480 offers free on-line therapy from Eliza! (One of the early products of artificial intelligence research.)

Electronic Syllabus

May 2, Monday: Introduction

Introductions, Formation of Groups, & Workshop Classroom Website

Planning the Course, Individual and Group Expectations, formation of groups

1. Register for your own website at WMU homepages.

2. Think about the role of technology in the future of English teaching. a) View the streaming video Teaching English through Technology website. b) be sure you know what each page of a classroom website should contain. c) Examine the Growing up Digital site: What is the "N-generation and what are its characteristics?"

3. Join and respond to questions on "English-Teacher."

May 4, Wednesday: Resources for Teaching Literature

Introduction to English Journal and Teaching Resources Web Site Workshop and Minilessons

1. Read carefully through the entire on-line syllabus, including all assignments. Bring any questions about these assignments to class.

2. Read three or more articles from back issues of the English Journal that relate to the theme of the student-led group you are in. You should subscribe to the English Journal and you can do so from its home website and view the current issue. NCTE members can read back issues of the English Journal at the NCTE website on-line using their membership number as a password. Back issues are also in the Sangren Library under the call number PE1 .E5. (Between Waldo (all issues before 1980) and Sangren (issues after 1980) we have back issues to 1912 when the English Journal began publication--fascinating reading in the history of secondary English teaching!) You can conduct an ERIC search for articles on specific topics by setting "English Journal" as the "source."

3. Find and examine three or more secondary English lesson plans available on the web relevant to your theme that you consider to be well crafted. There are many sources for Language Arts lesson plans on the web. Try Outta Ray's Head, the Collaborative Lesson Archive, NCTE's Notes Plus (subscribers only), New York Times Lesson Plan Archive, Lesson Planz.com, Cyberguides, Lesson Plans Page, ERIC, etc.

4. Write a one paragraph description of each of the journal articles and lesson plans that you have selected. These paragraphs will be a required part of the page on your website for teachers or colleagues. Post these paragraphs on "English-Teacher."

3. Study closely web resources for teachers and students. Look at Web Research sites and on-line Literature Resources, including BeyondBooks and other sites that support literature scholarship. Seek out webpages and websites that you would like to link to for your teaching website. Choose a website that you think might be especially useful and post a brief description of the site and its URL on "English-Teacher."

May 9, Monday: Reader Response

1) Present Individual Teaching Website to the Class

2) Read You Gotta BE the Book and respond to "English-Teacher."

Multiple Choice Test on You Gotta BE the Book

(I assume you have already know about the Fry Readability Graph from prior education classes. Study the National Middle School Association site and join the organization.)

May 11, Wednesday: Cultural Studies

1) Read Literature and Lives and respond to "English-Teacher."

Multiple Choice Test on Literature and Lives

2) Examine the NCTE/IRA Standards for the English Language Arts and Michigan English Language Arts Content Standards. Identify standards that support a social justice approach to teaching literature.
