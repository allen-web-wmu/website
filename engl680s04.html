English 680, Spring 2004

Advanced Methods of Teaching Literature

This graduate seminar will address the theory and practice of the teaching of literature at secondary and university levels. We will examine the historical development of literature teaching in English, the inclusion of multicultural materials and perspectives, the development of best instructional practices, the use of internet technology, and the emergence of literature teachers as professional leaders.

The final project of the course will be the development of a thematic curriculum for literature teaching drawing on a reflective, multicultural understanding of curriculum and best-practice teaching strategy. The course will be prepared to be taught using an interactive teaching website and will include internet learning activities such as webquests, on-line archives, electronic conferencing, etc. Streaming video explain the concept of the classroom website at "Teaching with Technology" page of the Teaching English through Technology website. For one of the literature teaching technology projects that you plan to do you will have a team of advanced undergraduate students working with you and under your direction.

The class will be taught in a wireless laptop classroom and will experiment with a variety of new technologies. Our class will be organized by this on-line syllabus that will also serve as an electronic, hyperlinked text book. We will have an electronic computer conference that will provide a forum for examination of course goals, content, activity, and methodology at nicenet.org. To participate, you will need sign up for the class named Teaching Literature (key number H89643T84). We will share this electronic conference with a section of English 480, that will allow seminar members both to engage in discussion and to serve as mentors/responders to aspiring teachers. My web site is an additional resource and a gateway to a variety of internet resources for English teachers. Bring floppy disks to class so you can retain copies of your work. Before the end of the course, I urge you to burn a copy of your websites and webquest on to a CD ROM. Help for technology projects can be had at the IT Center (WMU teachers) and the IT Lab (WMU students).

Students are expected to join the National Council of the Teachers of English or the Modern Language Association and attend a professional conference, such as the MCTE Bright Ideas Conference, Saturday March 20.

Class participation is vital in 680, missing classes may lower the grade and missing more than 3 classes may lead to failing. This class will follow WMU academic honesty policies. If at any point in the semester if you feel stress, English 680 offers free on-line therapy from Eliza! (One of the early products of artificial intelligence research.)

Literary Theory, Cultural Studies, and English Teaching

"Teaching About MLK Jr. and the Civil Rights Movement"

Internet Resources for Teaching

1. Examine webresources for teaching literature. That includes: 1. Web Research ; 2. Teaching Resources; 3. Literature Resources (we will examine archives more extensively later in the course); 4.On-line Lesson Plan sites such as Outta Ray's Head, the Collaborative Lesson Archive, NCTE's Notes Plus,New York Times Lesson Plan Archive, Lesson Planz.com, Cyberguides, Lesson Plans Page, ERIC , Encarta, etc. 5. Ezines. You could start with the On-line Library Ezine Index, Babel, and look at Amanda Warren's Ezine suggestions. Leading Discussion

1. "Questioning Behaviors" (from Making the Journey by Leila Christenbury, 1994, "Managing Recitation and Discussion" (chapter 10) from Secondary Classroom Management (McGraw Hill 1996).
2. Examine Discussion Analysis web page. 

March 1-5 WMU Spring Recess

Thursday, Mar. 11
On-Line Archives

1. Carefully examine an on-line literary archive that you might be able to use in your curriculum. Some of these archives are actually collections of archives--explore to find an archive you would like to work with. Conceive of a creative and meaningful lesson plan for students to study or use this archive. Sample archives: TeachEnglish Literary Archives Project Gutenberg, Early English Books On-line, Humanities Text Initiative, other on-line text resources. Develop a lesson plan for student to use this archive that requires close examination of the archive and higher level thinking.
2. Observe and videotape a classroom discussion of at least 30 minutes. Analyze the discussion based on what you already know and have learned this week. Use at least two instruments from Good and Brophy. Write up your analysis of the discussion to turn in on paper and disk. (minimum 4 pages)

 

Thursday, Mar. 18
Literature and Virtual Reality Environments:
Moos, Games, Live Journals

1. Look at the resources for building electronic classroom communities. Study Robert Rozema's resource on educational MOOs, especially the section on frequently asked questions. Choose an existing on-line MOO, or imagine a MOO that you would like to create in relation to a particular work of literature in your curriculum, describe the moo in a couple of paragraphs on the confer.
2. Look at a variety of on-line blogging sites. That could include: LiveJournal.com, Blogger.com, Look at the Live Journal of one of my former students, Bill Briger. Consider the possibility of using blogging for fictional characters.
3. Optional: Study high immersion games, on-line, playstation 2, x-box, etc. such as Everquest, Grand Theft Auto: Vice City, Myth, Myst, and think about how you might engage students in examining these activities as literature.

MCTE "Bright Ideas Conference" Lansing

Archeology of the Literature Curriculum

1. Read: Macaulay's "Minute" on Indian Education, Viswanathan "Currying Favor," Hawkes "Swisser-Swatter", Applebee "The Birth of a Subject," and Ngugi "Literature in Schools."


Thursday, April 1

Examining Historical Contexts and Canons

1. Read "American Literature a Comparative Discipline" by Paul Lauter
2. Undertake library research on a selected literary period with the goal to a) analyze the presentation of the canon of literature from that period (look at textbooks, anthologies, guides to literary study, special teaching editions of canonical works, and on-line syllabi) and b) Gather resources to develop cultural studies approaches to the period. Prepare to present your findings to the class.
3. Post completed webquest on your website and put a hyperlink to it on the confer.

Drawing on the website and reading, on the confer describe instructional models that you find relevent to the curriculum you are creating and list several reader-response approaches to teaching literature that you might like to use in your curriculum. (Though the expectations are higher for 680, some tips for this assignment can be found on the discussion analysis page for 480.)
