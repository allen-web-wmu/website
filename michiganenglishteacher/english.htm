After public review the current standards will be reviewed by
Achieve.org. Who are they and what might they do?

Achieve is a national organization associated with the American Diploma Project (ADP) and the Educational Testing Service. They have been used by many states and will be used by Michigan, to review state standards.

The standards that Achieve has endorsed in the past are based on grade-level-by-grade-level content specificity that severely limits teacher freedom and dumbs down the language arts curriculum. Achieve and the American Diploma Project hold out the Indiana state standards as a model for other states.

Good teachers view their classroom as a laboratory and they experiment with what and how they teach every year.  Teachers must have the freedom to change content and approach to respond to their students needs, interests, prior knowledge, and skills.  The proposed standards make this possible.

There are many things that secondary English teachers in Michigan need.  An enormous morass of picayune grade-level-by-grade-level standards to slog through is not one of them.  Far better are the proposed standards which, by respecting teachers and students, set a far higher standard for learning.

The proposed standards do not micromanaging the teaching in every high school English classroom in the state. This is not true of the step-by-step grade level content standards in Indiana that Achieve has approved which strip local control from school boards, school districts, high school administrators, high school English departments, and above all from the very English teachers who are best equipped to make judgments about the content and skills that will create high expectations for their students.  

Support the proposed standards that are NOT grade-level-by-grade-level and speak out now against grade-level-by-grade-level content expectations in Michigan.

Asking teachers to conform to elaborate grade-by-grade codified content standards that someone else has created insults their professional knowledge, undermines their passion, and devastates their effectiveness -- this could be insisted on by Achieve and that is why the current public review of the proposed standards is important. 

Learning and knowledge in secondary English language arts does not lend itself to standardization. Content knowledge and skills in language arts do not sequence like increasingly complex algebraic problems or geometric theorems.  A student who doesn’t understand A Tale of Two Cities may become utterly engaged with Hamlet or vice versa, and it would deaden the study of the works to try to find a set of abstracted “skills” that every student who reads A Tale of Two Cities or Hamlet supposedly “needs to know.”

The Indiana Academic Standards approved by Achieve are mindlessly and mind-numbingly redundant and repetitive.  For example at every grade level students are expected to study Greek, Latin, and Anglo-Saxon word roots.  This approach kills the joy, enthusiasm, and value of learning.   Excessively codified knowledge supposedly covering all areas in secondary language arts is impoverished knowledge.  Scripted language arts teaching is poor teaching.  Teachers who are personally engaged with their content, who are given the support and freedom to shape that content and connect it to the lives of their students and the issues that matter in today’s world in evolving, creative, personal, independent, unique ways are teacher who will inspire their students to learn. 

The ADP and Indiana Academic Standards elevate relatively trivial knowledge to a high level while downplaying rich and vital content.  For example, out of the 65 standards established for 9th grade, one standard asks students to “analyze the way in which a work of literature is related to the themes and issues of its historical period.”  This is a most important and central undertaking in literature study and should rightly be the focus of a great part of any language arts course.  Yet, because of the creation of comprehensive standards covering all possible subjects, this standard becomes 1/65 of the language arts learning that is supposed to happen in the 9th grade!

Yes, there are a great number of sub-skills that English teachers want their students to learn.  Indeed, there is no topic in language arts that has been more extensively researched over the last 100 years.  That research clearly and unequivocally points to the idea that skills need to be taught when the students are ready and in the context of meaningful learning.  The proposed standards support that view.

Creating comprehensive lists of every possible sub-skill and then identifying grade level by grade level as to precisely when they will be taught will undermine the learning of those skills. 

The ADP report and the grade by grade Indiana Academic Content Standards assume a comprehensive, sequential step-by-step model of learning in the English Language Arts.  This model is simply not appropriate to the content in question. 

The language arts is a complex field deeply connected to broad aspects of culture, historical and contemporary, and involving an enormous range of non-sequential skills and attitudes.  In the language arts it is not necessary for every student to march through the same content or skills in order to be outstandingly well prepared for college or the work place.

In the language arts a comprehensive coverage model where specific skills or content knowledge is supposed to be systematically touched upon will be less engaging and less meaningful than one where students go into great depth and complexity with a focused look at specific skills, authors, texts or themes.  Indeed, students steeped in and excited about a particular subset of content or skills are likely to be MORE attractive to colleges and work places than students who can march superficially through a supposedly comprehensive list of abstracted skills. 

The teaching of English depends greatly on the differential prior knowledge that each student brings to the classroom.  Teachers must have the freedom to select content and design curriculum that moves forward from the knowledge and skills of their students.  Uniform content standards in language arts will most of the time constitute “dumbed down,” too difficult, or irrelevant content. 

While these concerns are relevant at all grade levels, they are much more pressing at the high school level where the dispersion of student preparation and life experience is much greater, grade level by grade level.  While it is not uncommon to find students in the sixth grade that are three years behind or ahead of grade level in reading, it is all the more common to find students in the eleventh grade who are six years behind or ahead of grade level.  Forcing students and teachers into a standards driven curriculum straight jacket will lead those who are behind to become farther behind and those who are ahead to be held back.  

The Indiana Academic Standards treat language arts content as fixed and non-controversial, yet there is not a national consensus about either the skills or the content of secondary English, or about literary or cultural knowledge for that matter.  These are hotly contested areas.  English professors at the finest universities are in battle over these subject areas and public commentators are joining in.  It is not right, nor will it lead to effective instruction, to try to bleed the life and controversy out of English language arts by the creation of supposedly value neutral standards. 

While every English teacher needs to make book lists, advocate student reading, and help students select quality literature with personal and cultural relevance, lists of state approved books that English teachers must or should teach will not forward language arts learning in Michigan.  Dissertations could be written on the limitations of the reading list that accompanies the Indiana Academic Standards. 

The ADP and Indiana Academic Standards constrain secondary English teachers and attempt to silence English students from joining into the controversies of America’s cultural conversation when precisely what needs to be done is to invite students into that conversation. 

The ADP and Indiana standards do not envision an evolving, dialogic curriculum, but one that is static, mechanical, and highly repetitive.  Neither “politically correct” nor “politically neutral” approaches should be foisted onto Michigan students.  Instead, teachers and students must be free to explore controversial materials in ways that are sensitive and contextualized to local students, classrooms, and communities. 

The real secret to education in any field, and especially in the language arts, is the passion of the teacher to teach content that they love and know about.  The kind of comprehensive, narrowly conceived grade-level-by-grade-level content standards adopted by the state of Indiana and held out as a model for Michigan will undermine the passion and commitment of the teacher to the content.  The kind of content standard envisioned by ADP and Indiana is devastating to learning in the language arts.

The grade level content standards that Achieve envisions are clearly a prelude to state grade-level-by-grade-level MEAP testing.  Grade-level-by-grade-level standardized testing will not improve language arts education in Michigan.  The fact that evidence is mounting nationwide on this topic is indicated by the failure of congress to pass President Bush’s NCLB for high schools, even though it was supposedly the highest domestic priority of the administration. 

Why should we in Michigan rush ahead on an agenda that is clearly failing nation wide?  If you examine the ADP website you will see that Michigan currently rates ABOVE the average of other states on all of their indicators of school success and ABOVE Indiana in terms of number of students graduating from high school. 

In schools and classrooms the focus on mind-numbing and stultifying grade-level-by-grade-level standards and testing wastes valuable instructional time and turns students off of the value of learning.  Believe me, it is the rare high school student who is motivated to learn because he or she wants to do well on a standardized test.  To repeat, students are motivated to learn by teachers who are passionate and knowledgeable about their subject and by teachers who respect them as learners. 

In creating standards like those in ADP or Indiana we will not be respecting the teachers as knowledgeable about their content area and skillful at teaching, we will, in fact, be profoundly undermining them.  Just as students are not motivated to learn because they are told that something will be on a standardized test, so teachers are not motivated to teach when someone else, someone working through the Michigan Department of Education, for example, tells them what their discipline is or what or how they should teach it. 

If Michigan wants to set a standard for the nation, if the legislature and the governor want to create a system of education to be a national model, lets help them put forward ways to truly support secondary English teachers and students. Ways that go beyond creating standards!

Dr. Allen Webb
