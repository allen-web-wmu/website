What do teachers have to say about this?

Dr. Webb,

BRAVO!!! I wish you all the luck in the world putting this thing together. It sounds like what is coming may be a nightmare.

I first taught in Texas in the late 80s when everyone was on the same page. I left at semester break because I simply couldn't do that. Now I am reaching the end of my career and it is rearing its ugly head again.

At my high school last August on the first day of school, our principal informed us that we would all teach the same skills, the same content, the same test on the same day. I thought I had moved back to Texas. I asked him what his reasoning was and he said it was NCLB and EdYES that required this. We did not take this sitting down and called MDE for clarification. They told us this was not what was intended. Nevertheless, we were given this same directive again this spring at our last department meeting -- that we should all do the same thing each and every day. It has taken the wind out of our teaching as you suggest.

I am sure I am not alone in this situation. It appalls me that we are returning to an out-dated, obviously poor model, that is sure to have little success. Students are not cookie cutters, and neither are we. It is ridiculous to imagine that English is like math or science. Although the skills do build on each other, no one has ever asked me what a gerund was since my last linguistics class. As for outside the educational world, I have never been asked. It is not as if foreshadowing and its nuances are a topic of everyday conversatoin, but it is part of what makes a good scary movie. Most people would be able to identify the characteristics of foreshadowing in story and film, but may not be able to identify for term itself. Unlike in math where 2 and 2 are always 4, it is not necessary to memorize the terms but to become immersed in them and learn to love literature.

I did not mean to rant but this is an issue that has been near and dear to me for all of my teaching life. If I can help in any specific way, please let me know.

Sue Kohfeldt, June 14, 2005
