Michigan English Teacher.com

 

What is happening?

Currently a group of five college professors and one high school teacher are being paid by the Michigan Department of Education to decide what every teacher will teach and what every child will learn in every high school English classroom in the state from the 9th to the 12th grade. This group holds its first meetings in Lansing June 16 &17.

From Allen Webb's Letter to the Committee, June 9:

We are told that the American Diploma Project (ADP) and the academic standards of the State of Indiana will serve as models for our work.

Proceeding to create grade level content standards for high school Language Arts, the focus for this committee, without examining the fundamental assumptions that underlie the ADP report that is supposed to guide our work would be irresponsible and unprofessional.  We need to stop and ask what will be the effect of the standards it is proposed we create on the learning of students in language arts classes in Michigan?

The thesis of the American Diploma Project (ADP) is that the problem with American education is that high school diplomas do not indicate that students are prepared for success in college or work.  Yet, even their own data does not bear out their conclusions.  The problem is clearly not that ALL diplomas are meaningless.  

In Michigan the problem is not that the public schools have low standards.  The problem is that in key areas, especially low income areas in the inner cities and some rural areas, students are not given enough help to overcome the handicaps they face and they fall farther and farther behind until they drop out in enormous numbers.  No wonder these students are alienated from our public schools.  These are the children who are “left behind” and their situation rightly should be the focus of our and public attention.

Research shows that what these students need is smaller school settings, more personal connection with teachers and schools, and more support to meet the teachers and schools’ expectations.  To achieve these goals means that schools for poor and low income students need more resources, more teachers, smaller classes, more support personnel.  Why should it be a mystery to anyone in a democracy that schools serving students from low socioeconomic groups need MORE resources than school serving wealthy students, yet, of course, they receive LESS -- and the problem further compounds. 

Legislators and policy makers may think they can paper over this reality with “quick fix” and “inexpensive” standards and testing, but despite what the testing companies who stand to profit claim, the research is clear that such supposedly “cheap” efforts will worsen the situation for those “left behind”  and bring down the quality of education for all students.

Yes, there are schools, and tracks within schools, where standards need to be higher.  The ADP project is not completely wrong about that.  But simply legislating one size fits all standards, for all students, in all schools, particularly “fine grained” “year-by-year” content standards, will lead to MORE SCHOOL FAILURE, not less.  Sadly it is not even cynical to remark that having the public schools fail is an intended goal for some of the framers of NCLB who want charter, religious, and private schools instead.  (Why else set average yearly progress standards that are, in fact, statistically impossible for any school to meet in the long run?  Moreover, the record on charter schools is poor (especially in Michigan which has gone ahead and authorized charter schools willy-nilly) – they are not going to “raise standards” either.))  It is not gratuitous that I link ADP and NCLB; they are based on the same premises and approaches... ]

There are meaningful models of content standards in Language Arts.  Those models specifically reject grade level content standards and fine grained content and skill analysis. 

In fact, Michigan already has sound content standards for the English language arts!  The existing Michigan language arts content standards and benchmarks were developed over several years with the involvement of a number of classroom teachers. There are ten standards with 43 different specific benchmarks for content knowledge and skills at the high school level.  High school level language arts is coordinated with lower grade levels.  The existing standards are enough without trying to further hamstring teachers.  In Indiana there are 65 standards for the 9th grade language arts alone!  Talk about deadening education with an overload of scripted specificity! 

Michigan’s existing standards are similar to, though more detailed and specific than the standards created by the National Council of the Teachers of English and the International Reading Association.  These two enormous language arts teacher organizations with over 140,000 members and to which ALL of the leading researchers in the field of language arts education in American belong, worked closely together for years to develop standards that were sufficiently focused to give guidance and create high expectations and, yet, sufficiently open to facilitate the kind of freedom that teachers and students need to excel. 

Michigan’s existing standards, modeled on NCTE and IRA approaches, demonstrate an understanding of language arts education and draw on efforts by leading researchers and scholars to create effective, workable, and high achieving standards.  Our state’s standards should not be bull dozed by us or by politicians or bureaucrats in the Michigan Department of Education eager to jump on a bandwagon that has already gone by, with results that will be crippling for education in Michigan. 

Micromanaging the teaching in every high school English classroom in the state is not and should not be the function of our committee, the state legislature, the Michigan Department of Education or the governor’s office.  The step-by-step grade level content standards in Indiana that are proposed as a model for Michigan strip local control from school boards, school districts, high school administrators, high school English departments, and above all from the very English teachers who are best equipped to make judgments about the content and skills that will create high expectations for their students.   

Asking teachers to conform to elaborate grade-by-grade codified content standards that someone else has created insults their professional knowledge, undermines their passion, and devastates their effectiveness.  The problem is not lack of standards.  The problem is, or will be, the imposition of standards.  As an expert in this field, I tell you with confidence that learning and knowledge in secondary English language arts does not lend itself to standardization.

The very idea that a committee of five college professors and one public school teacher, no matter how eager or how well-qualified, will, in two months time, create grade-level-by-grade-level content standards for every high school English class and every high school language arts student in the state of Michigan is, in itself, absurd, appalling, and undemocratic. 

The Indiana Academic Standards that we are told is our model is mindlessly and mind-numbingly redundant and repetitive.  For example at every grade level students are expected to study Greek, Latin, and Anglo-Saxon word roots.  This approach kills the joy, enthusiasm, and value of learning.   Excessively codified knowledge supposedly covering all areas in secondary language arts is impoverished knowledge.  Scripted language arts teaching is poor teaching.  Teachers who are personally engaged with their content, who are given the support and freedom to shape that content and connect it to the lives of their students and the issues that matter in today’s world in evolving, creative, personal, independent, unique ways are teacher who will inspire their students to learn. 

The ADP and Indiana Academic Standards elevate relatively trivial knowledge to a high level while downplaying rich and vital content.  For example, out of the 65 standards established for 9th grade, one standard asks students to “analyze the way in which a work of literature is related to the themes and issues of its historical period.”  This is a most important and central undertaking in literature study and should rightly be the focus of a great part of any language arts course.  Yet, because of the creation of comprehensive standards covering all possible subjects, this standard becomes 1/65 of the language arts learning that is supposed to happen in the 9th grade!

Yes, there are a great number of sub-skills that English teachers want their students to learn.  Indeed, there is no topic in language arts that has been more extensively researched over the last 100 years.  That research clearly and unequivocally points to the idea that skills need to be taught when the students are ready and in the context of meaningful learning. 

Creating comprehensive lists of every possible sub-skill and then identifying grade level by grade level as to precisely when they will be taught will undermine the learning of those skills.  Michigan’s existing content standards provide an appropriate, flexible, and intellectually defensible framework to support Michigan’s teachers in their efforts to provide students with language arts skills.

The Indiana Academic Standards treat language arts content as fixed and non-controversial, yet there is not a national consensus about either the skills or the content of secondary English, or about literary or cultural knowledge for that matter.  These are hotly contested areas.  English professors at the finest universities are in battle over these subject areas and public commentators are joining in.  It is not right, nor will it lead to effective instruction, to try to bleed the life and controversy out of English language arts by the creation of supposedly value neutral standards. 

While every English teacher needs to make book lists, advocate student reading, and help students select quality literature with personal and cultural relevance, lists of state approved books that English teachers must or should teach will not forward language arts learning in Michigan.  Dissertations could be written on the limitations of the reading list that accompanies the Indiana Academic Standards. 

The ADP and Indiana Academic Standards constrain secondary English teachers and attempt to silence English students from joining into the controversies of America’s cultural conversation when precisely what needs to be done is to invite students into that conversation. 

The ADP and Indiana standards do not envision an evolving, dialogic curriculum, but one that is static, mechanical, and highly repetitive.  Neither “politically correct” nor “politically neutral” approaches should be foisted onto Michigan students.  Instead, teachers and students must be free to explore controversial materials in ways that are sensitive and contextualized to local students, classrooms, and communities. 

Speaking as professor of English, and I can assure you that almost any professor of English would agree, the standards that Indiana has adopted are sophomoric; they are the work of people who truly do not understand the content and discipline of English as well as they should, but, acting as “wise fools” they are willing to tell others what to do.

We are far smarter than that in Michigan.  In Michigan we greatly value the expertise of those in the academic disciplines (in our state all secondary students must major in the content area of English and the professors of English education are content area specialists usually in English departments).  Indeed, by any measure, Michigan is one of the nation’s leaders in English education.  Heaven forbid we copy cat an inferior approach.

Good teachers view their classroom as a laboratory and they experiment with what and how they teach every year.  Teachers must have the freedom to change content and approach to respond to their students needs, interests, prior knowledge, and skills.  Supporting that kind of freedom should be the task of our committee and the educational and political leaders of the state.
