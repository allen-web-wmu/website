What do secondary English teachers really want?

The English teachers of Michigan want the freedom and respect to develop creative, meaningful, and challenging curriculum. They want students to engage in significant, extensive, and regular reading and writing.  They believe this happens best under the guidance of a teacher who is passionate about their curriculum and inspired by the joy and value of reading and writing. Michigan English teachers are committed to fostering the the skills and cultural knowledge necessary for school and work place success and democratic participation in our state, country, and world.

High school English teachers need the Michigan Department of Education, the legislature, and the governor’s office to respect their professionalism, their ability to develop curriculum, and their teaching abilities. They do not want the state to mandate curriculum, reading lists, or create overly specific standards and curriculum expectations. They do not want to march their students through narrow textbook approaches, cook book knowledge, mere lists of terms to memorize, or simplistic learning strategies. They want authentic, high quality, and professionally current teaching and learning.

High school English teachers need:

1)  Time to read their student’s papers.  Many teach way too many students, often 5 classes and 150 students a day.  (150 students writing 2 pages per day, equals 300 pages of student writing to read per day, 1500 pages of writing per week!)  They need decent working conditions.

2) Funds to purchase the books that they passionately want to teach.  Many teachers and students do not have the books they need, and there is no program in the state to help teachers get books! 

3) Programs and personnel in schools that will work closely with them to help students with poor preparation, low skills or special needs so that these students can complete the meaningful work that their English teacher assign.  These students do not need workbooks of isolated knowledge or skills.

4) Time and support to work with colleagues and other professionals.  They need time to work together. They are open to continued professional learning opportunities, not of the prepackaged educational-expert-knows-all type, but instead led by state and national professional English teacher organizations.  Secondary English teachers need to be in dialogue with each other and with university scholars and resource people in their communities.  A shinning example is the outstanding National Writing Project sites in Michigan based on "teachers teaching teachers" model.  (The legislature and the governor don’t know about education in Michigan and its national leadership and capacity for change if they don’t know about Michigan’s writing projects sites.) 

5) Technology support that addresses serious language arts learning, not the simplistic “laptop for every sixth grader” approach, but one that specifically targets content driven innovators who are, in turn utilized to serve as mentors to other teachers, on a teachers teaching teachers model.

6) On-going professional education.  There is an extensive body of contextualized literature on how to teach writing and reading well in English at the secondary level, including books specifically on how to teach to NCTE /IRA standards, and those books and resources need to become starting points for teacher conversations about teaching in Michigan and around the country.  I am confident when I say Michigan has the finest set of university graduate programs in English education of any state in America.  Teachers need support to participate in our state’s outstanding graduate programs.  Legislators and the Michigan Department of Education need to recognize and support those programs.

Simply mandating new standards, even if they are high quality, does not represent a real effort to improve education. Forcing grade-level-by-grade-level or course-by-course curriculum written at the state level or mandated by the state legislature on to teachers and students undermines meaningful learning. So does marching students through standardized testing while ignoring the much richer and more important assessment that happens everyday in the classroom.

Let's utilize the new language arts standards, and then move on to real educational reform!

Dr. Allen Webb
