Recommendation Letters

 

If I have sent you to this page then you can know that I would be delighted to write you a recommendation letter. Though no two letters are the same, all letters that I write are strongly positive. I try to look to your strengths and put them forward in a way that will make it hard for a future employer not to give you an interview or for a graduate or study abroad program not to accept you.

I write letters in the order that they are requested. I usually write them on an "electronic letterhead" and that allows me to email them to you (you can print), an employer, an on-line recommendation system such as Interfolio. To make a request please provide me with:

1) A current copy of your resume. (This helps me remember things about you and, by referring to specifics on your resume it helps me present you and indicate that I know you well.) I won't usually give you advice on your resume unless you have made extensive efforts to follow my resume advice and have set an appointment to meet with me one-on-one.

2) Some notes from you on what you did in my class. If you led an activity or group which group did you lead? What texts did you use? What activities did you organize? If you published a website, what is the address? (Should be on your resume!) If you wrote papers, what were they about? Any other specific accomplishments that I should know about or be reminded of? I will probably remember all of this, especially once I am reminded a bit, but I want the specifics again from you so that I will get the letter right.

3) Any other information or forms I need to write the letter. Often graduate schools require that a specific form accompany the letter--be sure you have signed this form in the appropriate place. If you want my letter to emphasize something specific, let me know. It is a good idea to tell me who else is writing letters for you so that my letter can compliment theirs. (If I am the only English professor, for example, then I can try to be sure I speak specifically to your English strengths.) If you are an aspiring teachers you may have a midterm or final evaluation of intern teaching; providing me with a copy can help strengthen the letter. Etc.

Writing recommendation letters is a task I take seriously. Depending on the letter, it takes me between one and two hours to write each letter, sometimes longer. I try to write well, get all details correct, and organize it on the page so that it is attractive. It is hard to know exactly how long it will take from the time you request your letter until the time I am able to write and send it to you. A quick turnaround is two weeks. If there is some kind of emergency need for a letter immediately, let me know. In this circumstance I can sometimes jump your letter to the head of the list.

Normally, I will send the letter to you. You will have a chance to read the whole letter and decide if you want to include it in your application materials. If you notice mistakes, let me know and I can change and reprint the letter. If you feel, based on looking at all of the letters in your file, that my letter should, perhaps, change its emphasis in some way you can ask me if I would be comfortable making that change. If you need a copy for your file at the placement office at WMU or elsewhere, it is your responsibility then to see that the original or a copy gets to the placement office--or wherever it needs to be.

If I write a letter for you I expect that you will let me know what happens. Did you get the job? Did you get into graduate school? Did you get the assistantship? Etc. Stay in touch and let me know what you are up to--this can be useful to you in future when you need another letter for whatever reason.

I wouldn't be writing for you if I didn't think you were an excellent candidate. Good luck!
