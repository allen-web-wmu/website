Webquests

Webquests are an exciting way to bring the resources of the internet into classroom teaching and they are ideal for reader response activities and cultural studies projects. There are many sites on the internet that support webquests -- and many "webquests" that are actually something less, mere "hot lists," or "treasure hunts," or "scrapbooks." (The Filamentality "Activity Formats" page defines these terms pretty well.)

Perhaps the best resource for webquests is the page created by Bernie Dodge at San Diego State University. Study carefully The Webquest Page. Begin by reading carefully through at least four or five examples --there are many for middle school and high school language arts. Look at quests at different levels. Some quests are clearly better than others. I like those that involve collaboration, have a clearly defined and creative final project, (Look for, instance, at the Odyssey Theme Park) and that involve a close and careful understanding of the text (Look at the Heart of Darkness Webquest ).

Webquests are a great way to create a cultural studies project around a work of literature. There are not very many webquests that take the ideas of cultural studies and apply them to literature--create one that will become a model to others.

As you prepare to build your own web quest, examine Bernie Dodge's training materials--within this the "building blocks" link is essential, but look also at the more sophisticated information..

Remember, the technology is a means to an end, not the end in itself; think carefully about what you want students to learn. A webquest doesn't have to rely solely on the web, use libraries and other resources. Learning how to research is one of the most important educational skills.
